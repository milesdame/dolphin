    • Introduction to Dolphins!

    Dolphins, a mammal in the family of toothed whales, live in both freshwater (like rivers and some lakes) and seas. Within the family of dolphins, there is a lot of variation. Some dolphins are small, agile, and stay close to the surface to take advantage of the sunlight. Other dolphins are huge, up to 10 tons, and evolved to have a layer of blubber to help keep them warm when diving deep for prey. All dolphins are carnivores. They like to eat fish and squid, and some of those larger species might hunt smaller mammals. Dolphins live in pods with their families.

    Though not common, some cultures still hunt dolphins for food. For example, cultures in Japan and the Faroe Islands will engage in drive hunts of dolphins, herding them into a contained area to harvest. Aside from hunting, dolphins also face a threat from the fishing industry. Dolphins are often caught as bycatch in fishing nets. Like many species globally, dolphins are also experiencing the effects of human development. Habitat loss, changing migration patterns of prey, and changing climate patterns all affect dolphin populations.

    Dolphins are known to be very social, both with other dolphins and with humans! Dolphins live in pods of a few to hundreds of other dolphins. They hunt together and will help injured dolphins by supporting them to the surface to breathe or bringing them food to help heal. With each other, dolphins teach their young how to hunt and even use tools, can remember each other's unique, name-like sounds, and will jump and play with each other. Dolphins have also been known to express grief over the death of a calf or pod member. With humans, dolphins have been observed swimming in circles around surfers to protect them from nearby sharks. They are also popular as attractions at water parks, where visitors can swim and play with dolphins. However, this kind of entertainment is often stressful to the dolphins and the swim tanks may be insufficient habitat for them.
