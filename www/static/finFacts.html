Fin Facts

Dolphins are highly intelligent: Dolphins have large brains relative to their body size, and they display complex behaviors like problem-solving, using tools, and even teaching each other.

They use echolocation: Dolphins emit sounds that bounce off objects, allowing them to "see" their environment through sound. This helps them navigate and hunt in murky waters.

Dolphins have names: Dolphins use specific whistles to identify each other, which scientists refer to as "signature whistles"â€”essentially, they call each other by name!

They are fast swimmers: Dolphins can reach speeds of up to 37 mph (60 km/h) when swimming, making them some of the fastest animals in the ocean.

Dolphins love to surf: Wild dolphins have been observed riding waves just for fun, often seen surfing alongside boats or in the wake of large ships.

They sleep with one eye open: Dolphins rest one half of their brain at a time while the other half remains awake to monitor the environment for predators or threats.

Dolphins have strong social bonds: They live in groups called pods and are known for their strong friendships, often seen playing, hunting, and protecting each other.

They help humans in the wild: There are documented cases of dolphins rescuing humans from shark attacks and guiding stranded people back to shore.

They can hold their breath for long periods: Dolphins can stay underwater for up to 15 minutes, but they usually surface to breathe every few minutes.

Dolphins are great imitators: They can mimic the movements and vocalizations of other animals and even human behaviors, which is a sign of advanced learning ability.

They communicate with clicks, whistles, and body language: Dolphins use a variety of sounds and movements to communicate, including tail slaps, leaps, and jaw claps.

Dolphin babies are called calves: Calves are born tail-first to prevent drowning, and they stay close to their mothers for protection and nursing.

Dolphins can recognize themselves in mirrors: They are one of the few animals, along with great apes and elephants, that show signs of self-awareness.

They are playful and creative: Dolphins have been seen blowing bubble rings, playing with seaweed, and even playing "catch" with fish.

There are over 40 species of dolphins: While most dolphins live in saltwater, there are also a few species of river dolphins that live in freshwater.
